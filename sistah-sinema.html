    Oral History by Deidre McPherson


      Founder of Sistah Sinema, Cleveland

    Sistah Sinema was founded in 2011 by Isis Asare in Seattle, WA as a space for dialogue and representation of Queer Women of Color through film. Sistah Sinema’s monthly film screenings (held by chapters across the country and globe) were accompanied by panel discussions, guest speakers, and other events to connect and create space for QWOC community members, activists, and organizations.

    In 2012, Deidre McPherson founded Sistah Sinema, Cleveland while working a full-time job in marketing. This work and experience coincided with her eventual career change into arts programming; first as Curator of Public Programs at MOCA Cleveland, and currently as Director of Public Programs at The Cleveland Museum of Art. We begin by discussing the early days of Sistah Sinema, planting roots, and building community.


    DEIDRE: Originally, Sistah Sinema was on the first Friday of every month, and we did that for the first two years. I still had a full-time job and had just migrated back to Cleveland. I boomeranged back, I left Cleveland for about 7-10 years and I moved back here to work at the Cleveland Orchestra, and I didn’t know anybody. All my friends that I grew up were moving and working in other cities. I was obsessed with this film, Pariah, and I saw someone was doing a screening of it in another city. This film was important—this was like the first film coming out that features a queer woman of color as the lead character, and it’s about her experience. And I thought, I wish there were a space where queer women of color could discuss a film after a screening. I thought that was so important because I didn’t have that growing up. There wasn’t that representation. So for me, seeing that, I felt like I needed that in Cleveland.

    At the time, Phyllis Seven Harris was the board member for The LGBT Community Center of Greater Cleveland, and I had just met her through a meetup group for Black women with natural hair. And I was like ‘I need to be her friend. I don’t know any other queer women of color in Cleveland.’ When I got the idea to bring Sistah Sinema to Cleveland, Seven was the first person I called. And I said ‘Seven, I got this idea, it’s this thing called Sistah Sinema, it’s happening in Seattle, I talked to the founder, she said I could bring it to Cleveland, I don’t know any queer women of color [laughs] how am I going to build an audience for this thing?’ Seven opened up her home, she invited the most vocal queer women of color and BIPOC, so I could tell them about Sistah Sinema and to ask for their support. She was like ‘I support you, I see you, and this is important.’ She helped me build a market because I didn’t have those connections.

    Then I was like okay, I’m ready, what do I need to do? I need an email list. I need to make a press release... how do I make a press release? So I googled that. How do you make a flyer? How do you make preview slides, so that when your audience is waiting for the program to begin, they can see what’s going on in the community? What should the conversation be after the film? What are they key ideas? What are the takeaways? How can we relate this to our experience? Are there panelists? Are there performers? Are there speakers? Are there artists that we should hear from that relate to the subject matter of this film? The films were being curated and selected by the founder, Isis Asare, and I would pay a fee to show that film in Cleveland and break even on costs to show it and share it locally. Word of mouth grew, and I mean it was a multi-generational event. It was queer women of color, queen people of color from ages twenty to seventies. There was a woman in her seventies who would come, and she was just coming out to herself. Sistah Sinema was the first public event that she had ever been to where she was surrounded by queer people of color and acknowledging that she was a queer woman. She found community in her seventies in the same way I had just migrated back to Cleveland in adulthood and didn’t know anyone, but Seven helped lift me up and we created in this space.

    That’s how it grew. It was monthly for several years and then we reduced the frequency. It was very much a startup and we were trying to figure out what was successful. Sistah Sinema had chapters in Cleveland, Portland, Seattle, for a while we had Kingston, Jamaica, Charlotte NC, Oakland, I mean, there were chapters in Texas at one point, and I got a chapter started in Columbus because we had a panelist in Columbus. It grew organically. It was one thing to go to a club or a lounge, but I think there are more clubs geared toward gay men and queer men then there are women. I don’t know what it is, but there are articles on this. I think women cultivate spaces in our homes and different spaces that aren’t necessarily clubs. But Sistah Sinema became the other thing to do. We would experiment with having an individual facilitate a conversation after a film. One time we showed a film that was a romantic comedy and we created a dating game with contestants [laugh] so it was like this fun experience where we were trying to make love happen because it happened to be February. We’d get a film screening on Black lesbians of the Harlem Renaissance; there was all types of queerness and creativity and artistry happening in Harlem, and no one really talks about the queerness… we talk about Langston Hughes, we talk about the writers and the visual artists of that time, but we’re not talking about how many of those important voices were queer people of color. So we did a program on that and that was amazing.



    Black Lesbians of the Harlem Renaissance: Double-feature accompanied by live jazz music. (Sistah Sinema @ SPACES. December 21, 2013)


      Pushing QWOC Voices Forward in Contemporary Art Spaces

    DEIDRE: I somehow got connected to MOCA Cleveland when they were transitioning from their Carnegie location to opening a brand new building in University Circle. I met the director through a research project and I was working at the Cleveland Orchestra at the time. She was interviewing me about my going into visual arts and I ended up talking about Sistah Sinema. She reached out to me after I had participated in this research project and she was like ‘Hey, I’d love for you to come do a Sistah Sinema program when we open our new building in University Circle.’ And I was like really? Okay, I guess I see it, you’re a contemporary art museum, you’re interested in diversifying your audience, so you want to welcome us and create space for queer POC, and you’re looking at film as art. At the time I was doing Sistah Sinema programs in art galleries… I was doing it at SPACES, I was doing it at Negative Space Gallery, I was doing it at Waterloo Arts, doing it in all these creative spaces.


      Bumming Cigarettes, Tiona Nekkia McClodden. (Sistah Sinema @ MOCA. April 27, 2013)

    The film that we had at MOCA was Tiona McClodden's Bumming Cigarettes. It was a short film about a Black lesbian who has a conversation with a homeless man who was bumming a cigarette from her while she awaits her AIDS test results. Usually the AIDS conversation in films are focused on white men, this one was about a Black lesbian, and it was a film by a Black lesbian. I reached out to Case Western Reserve University’s LGBT Center and LGBT Center of Greater Cleveland and brought them in as sponsors. They helped pay for bringing the filmmaker, now-turned artist Tiona McClodden to Cleveland for the film screening at MOCA. So that took the programming up a notch. Here we are at this brand new contemporary art space, we brought the filmmaker from Philadelphia to Cleveland, and the filmmaker is in dialogue with the executive director of the AIDS Taskforce of Greater Cleveland. We tried to get everybody to the table so we could have a full house, so we could have an audience to have a conversation around this film, around AIDS, and we’re connecting this film with the mission of our city’s AIDS taskforce in a contemporary art museum. And Tiona McClodden today is a very important contemporary artist and emerging voice. So Tiona’s debut in Cleveland was a Sistah Sinema film, not an art installation, and that was such an honor.

    So after I got laid off [from the aforementioned marketing job], MOCA was like, 'oh yeah we think we got a job you might be interested in. Have you ever heard of being a curator of public programs?' That’s how I got into that, and I fell in love with it.

    I was loving the idea of creating a space for underrepresented audiences and voices who don’t have a seat at the table and are not considered a priority. To be part of making that cultural shift in what’s being presented and talked about. To ask; what are the voices leading the conversation? How are we connecting all these parts of our cultural ecosystem together in something that’s valuable, that people talk about, and they tell their friends about? I wanted this unique space where I was not the only queer person of color, where the conversation was one in which I could be my full self. That was something that I needed, that I craved, and that was universal for other queer women of color and queer people of color. There were definitely queer men and gender nonconforming people who came too.


      Not a Monolith

    DEIDRE: Other programs that were really important to me were ones where the film was about our trans brothers and sisters. For me, that was an education. When I came out at 28, I knew about my experience as a Black lesbian, but I didn’t know about the experience of trans people. And so having an opportunity to show a number of films that looked at the experiences of trans women and trans men—and we’d do it in conversation with leaders of trans activism in Cleveland, where they are leading a conversation so that we can stand in solidarity with them, so they can share resources with us, so that we can be true allies and raise visibility for the issues that we know are still prevalent—for me, that was growth. And for audiences, that was important.

    We had a wide representation of films that we were showing that helped keep Sistah Sinema going. The logo of Sistah Sinema was of a Black woman with an afro, but it really was about all queer BIPOC. The genre of films was also diverse. Whether it was a romantic comedy or a documentary (which were my favorite.) We had stories about the Latinx community, Southeast Asian and Indian voices, Indigenous people, there were Native American Queer Women Shorts. [Deidre takes a moment to read through the spreadsheet of SS films] … We had a zombie-love-story-musical, okay! [laughs]. The way the films were curated, it was important that we represent all queer women of color.

    There were really a range of experiences in Sistah Sinema too and that was so beautiful. There were women and queer people who had been aware of their identity and their orientation throughout their whole life, and then there were those who were late bloomers like me, I didn’t really come out to myself till I was 28, and then there was this one woman who was in her seventies. That brought me so much joy that she found this space. I don’t know how she found it, but she found it through the LGBT Center of Greater Cleveland. The Center was really instrumental in helping us reach new audiences. So somehow the word got out to this woman and she would come to these events, she didn’t have a car but people would pick her up and bring her. And there would be popup tables—there were people who would come to Sistah Sinema who would be like ‘hey could I have a table to sell my jewelry, to sell my new book?’ So there were queer women of color who were lifting me up so that I could create this platform, and I’m using this platform to lift other people up. That was a lesson for me too. Like yeah absolutely, come bring your art, share it with us, tell people about your work. Let’s support one another.


      A Grassroots Effort

    DEIDRE: One thing that came out of the screening at MOCA was that somebody had invited an LGBT community leader from Pittsburgh and another LGBT leader from Columbus, and that led to connections to queer women of color communities in those cities. So that’s how the Columbus chapter of Sistah Sinema got started. We organized a queer women of color picnic, where we had a caravan of folks from Cleveland that drove to Columbus to have fellowship and food with queer women of color in Columbus. There were probably about thirty or so folks all gathering together, and we tried to make that an annual thing.


      Out in the Night, Blair Dorosh-Walther. (Sistah Sinema @ CIFF. March 22, 2015)

    We made bumper stickers, we made buttons [laughs] we had a table at Pride. Every year for the Cleveland International Film Festival, the festival would have community partners that would essentially promote a film that connects to that nonprofit’s mission. So anytime the festival had a film in their schedule lineup about a queer woman of color, I would be the community partner on that film, Sistah Sinema Cleveland. And I would get to introduce that film at the screening and introduce people to Sistah Sinema. And then I had a table outside the film so I can meet people, get them on my email list and invite them to the next Sistah Sinema program. We were really grassroots… there was no marketing budget, I mean I made a little bit of money from programs, but tried to reinvest it in growing. For me it was a passion project, and with each film screening we were reaching a different community, whether it’s the Latinx community or other different ethnic and cultural groups.

    We started having food, food fellowship, people would be selling plates, so it became more than a movie. Like yeah, you’re coming to see a movie, but these are films that are not widely-distributed, you’re not gonna see these films at Regal Cinema. There was no Netflix, there was no Hulu. We didn’t have these independent channels where these independent films were being distributed the way they are today. So Sistah Sinema was IT! And the woman who started it, she was so entrepreneurial, she was thinking about how to make a whole channel of media. She had big aspirations and she was very forward-thinking. Of course I think the model for now, especially in a virtual world, would be very different.



      Placemaking as Necessity

    LEILA: Hearing more about Sistah Sinema as you go into detail, it’s just clearly such an important and necessary resource, and on so many levels. The representation of QWOC in film is extremely important in and of itself, and on top of that you’re creating this really diverse programing, room for discussion, and also the safe space—I keep thinking about this elderly woman who found her community there.


    DEIDRE: Yeah! When I first moved back to Cleveland I was not “out”. My parents go to a pretty conservative Black Baptist church and there’s an expectation that I go each week. And I’m like trying to find my legs in my city, like, I’m about to start this REALLY gay project [laughs]. I come out to my parents slightly before that because I was like, ok if I get into a relationship and I fall in love I’m going to come out to my parents. And that happened. And then I go to church with them and one of their friends goes “Heeeyyyy! You’re a star! I saw your film club in the newspaper, that’s great! Congratulations!” And my mom’s like... not sure how to feel about it. [laughs] My parents are amazing, they’re in their late seventies, they see how this work has given me life and lead to me doing what I do now at the museum, which you know, they’re really proud of it.

    My intent was not to be in leadership… I’m an organizer, I’m an activist type, that just happens when you organize things. And that was an interesting journey too. It was helpful for me to look back at other people who were organizing and doing comparable work in creating spaces for queer women of color. I thought about people like Sherry Bowman and other women before me who have created these spaces. I look to them with much respect, and I was thinking, how can I work with them? How can I learn from them? Seven was definitely a mentor and a good friend and people like Carmen Lane, who is now a much more active practicing artist but has always been an artist, was helping me in sharing their voice in Sistah Sinema programs.

    There was so much diversity in the queer people of color who came, and no other reason why we would have all met. There were relationships, people were hooking up, there were love matches, there were friendships, there were vacations taken, people made friends, I made friends, I made connections, that kept me coming, I think that kept people coming back. There are queer women of color working in every sector of our city, in technology, in medicine, in academia, in the arts, but we would’ve never crossed paths if there wasn’t this thing we were all going to and sharing our voices where we could connect with each other. I can go to church and get my healing, but I can’t be my full self. I can go to work, I work in a predominantly white institution—I love arts and culture, that’s what I have in common with these people—but I still can’t be my full self. They don’t really understand my queerness, my Blackness. Sistah Sinema was unique in that it filled that space, it was that third space where I’m seen and I can really talk about all these complexities, these challenges, these opportunities, these beautiful things about our identities as queer women of color.


      A Place in the Middle, Kumu Hina. (Sistah Sinema @ Waterlooo Arts. May 1, 2015)

    I do miss it. When I started organizing these events, that was not my 9-5, it was a passion project, but then my career transitioned and it became my career, organizing these events and programs. I did fewer and fewer Sistah Sinema events, but every once in a while I would be invited or asked to do something special. When Waterloo Arts decided to organize the first exhibition of trans artists in the state, they reached out like ‘Hey Deidre, would you be able to recommend any films we could show in support of this exhibition of trans artists?’ I had just had the opportunity to be a judge for short films for CIFF—me and two other people watched every single short film in the festival and we picked the winners. So I had just seen like five films out of a couple hundred that were about trans people. So I connected Waterloo Arts with CIFF and I said hi, this exhibition’s important, can we show some of these films in connection with the exhibition?

    Something else that I was really proud of was—are you familiar with Zanele Muholi?


    LEILA: Ooh yes.


    DEIDRE: Right? We showed Zanele Muholi’s Difficult Love in all Sistah Sinema chapters. So while I’m working at MOCA, Zanele Muholi was chosen as the endowed lecturer in contemporary art at CMA. And they were like, how can we get queer women of color to this event to see this endowed lecture? [laughs] We don’t know any. And someone was like ‘Wait, do you know Deidre McPherson? She works at MOCA, why don’t you reach out and ask her if she can help us bring that audience to Zanele Muholi’s lecture.’ So, I’m working at MOCA and I’m like well, have you seen her film Difficult Love? I suggested having a Sistah Sinema program at MOCA with Zanele Muholi on Friday night, and then encourage the audience to go hear her endowed lecture the next day at CMA. And they were like, Okay! [laughs] and people were like oh my god, I have an opportunity to meet Zanele Muholi from South Africa. You know, this photographer is internationally renowned.


      Difficult Love, Zanele Muholi. (Sistah Sinema @ CMA. February 6, 2016)
      Photo by Zanele Muholi, Courtesy of Michael Stevenson Gallery.

    So that was my introduction to CMA. And I think Zanele really appreciated that we had made that effort, that we had had an audience of what felt like family—It’s not just the typical white museum audience, there are actually queer women of color, BIPOC here because someone had the insight to reach out to Sistah Sinema. But those things don’t always happen. Predominantly white-led institutions don’t always know how to, or they fail to develop the audiences that come to these spaces. That was kinda how and why Sistah Sinema found a home in SPACES, and that I ended up on their board. I wanted to serve SPACES and be on their board because that’s where I had held the events most of the time. And it was benefitting them as well, because here I was bringing Sistah Sinema audiences to a contemporary arts space. This audience is not usually like ‘hey let’s go see contemporary art at SPACES,’ but even though we’re hosting Sistah Sinema events there was kinda like oh, maybe there’s some relevance or connection to actually coming back to the space. So that was I think why SPACES and MOCA found it beneficial. They wanted to connect with diverse audience and that’s how they recognized Sistah Sinema could do that.


      Holding Art Institutions Accountible

    LEILA: You would know much more about this than me, but it seems like art institutions are finally being held accountable, in a way that’s more urgent, to reckon with their own systemic racism.


    DEIDRE: Oh yeah. Everybody made the default social media posts standing in solidarity with the Black community. Whether it’s corporate or predominantly white institutions, we’ve seen all the statements. Now it’s like, what are you gonna do about it? The City of Cleveland, City of Akron, and City of Columbus have all declared racism a public health crisis. Three cities in Ohio. And there are institutions signing pledges that they commit to doing programming to help address fighting institutional and systemic racism, fighting this public health crisis. Not everybody’s ready to sign that pledge though, because a lot of institutions need to look at themselves and actually understand what it means to stand in solidarity. It doesn’t mean ‘Black people, we’re prayin’ for you.’ It means, ‘We’re going to take a look at ourselves and commit to doing better. We’re not going to be perfect, we’re going to make some mistakes, but we’re going to do the work and we’re going to look at our whiteness and how we have historically not created a welcoming space for anyone who wasn’t white. How we were not founded for everybody. But we now recognize that we need to be for everybody if we want to sustain ourselves.’ And that’s the right thing to do. Oh yeah, morals, values, that too, yeah [laughs].

    It’s been really interesting to look at different institutions, whether it’s the NFL or San Francisco MOMA or Cleveland Institutions, how there’s often a lack of humility around admitting historic missteps, admitting how historically we have not been inclusive. Very few are doing that and I think that’s essential in building trust. Because of the systems and the structures in place, that have been in place since the origins. Because we have predominantly white boards, white staff, white everything, and these are broken systems that need to be fixed. It’s going to take more than one person or one diverse staff member. It’s going to take really looking at and dismantling the systems that got us here in the first place. There’s a lot of education… I gotta educate myself too. Yes, I’m a Black queer woman, but I gotta decolonize myself, I’ve been working at predominantly white institutions and I don’t want to be elitist or leave anyone to feel left out. I know what it feels like to be left out for being different, for being othered. I want us arts administrators to continue the work, across our cultural, artistic, educational, civic, ecosystem in our city. I want us all to continue these conversations, to change policies at the organizational level. It’s not one event or one program that’s gonna completely fix or change the landscape.
